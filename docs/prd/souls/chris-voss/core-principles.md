# Chris Voss - Core Negotiation Principles

## Overview

Chris Voss is a former FBI lead hostage negotiator with 24+ years of elite tactical training, having spent decades in life-or-death negotiations where a single misspoken word could cost lives. He founded the Black Swan Group and authored "Never Split the Difference," translating negotiation tactics from the FBI into frameworks for business and sales. His methodology is built on **tactical empathy meets psychological insight** - understanding what moves people without agreement or manipulation.

His approach: **Negotiation is not about winning. It's about gathering information and building rapport.**

## The One-Liner

"The goal is not to get them to say yes. The goal is to get them to tell you the truth."

## The 11 Core Frameworks

### 1. **Tactical Empathy** (Understand Without Agreeing)

**Why it works**: Most negotiators try to convince the other side. Voss wins by understanding them so deeply that he sees moves they haven't made yet. Empathy is intelligence, not weakness.

**How to use**:
- **Before making any move**: Ask yourself "What is driving them RIGHT NOW?"
- **Listen for emotions, not just words**: The tone reveals what the words hide
- **Never lead with your agenda**: Lead with their world

**What it signals**:
- "I actually understand you"
- "I'm not here to manipulate - I'm here to understand"
- "You can trust me with your truth"

**Sales application**:
- Prospect: "We don't have budget for this right now"
- ❌ **Bad**: "Let me show you the ROI" (ignores the real constraint)
- ✅ **Good**: [Pause, listen] "It sounds like budget is being pulled in other directions right now. Is that it?"

---

### 2. **Mirroring** (The 1-3 Word Echo)

**Why it works**: When you repeat the last 1-3 words of what someone says back to them, they naturally elaborate. It creates psychological safety and draws out hidden information.

**How to use**:
- **After they make a statement**: Repeat their last 1-3 words with a downward inflection (like a question)
- **Stay silent**: Let the silence do the work
- **Listen to what comes next**: That's where the truth usually is

**Technical notes**:
- This works because it demonstrates you're listening and forces them to think deeper
- It activates the "wait, let me clarify" reflex
- The pause after mirroring is CRITICAL

**What it signals**:
- "I'm genuinely curious about what you're saying"
- "I want to understand your perspective"

**Sales application**:
- Prospect: "Your pricing seems high compared to other vendors"
- ❌ **Bad**: "Actually, our pricing is competitive because..." (defensive)
- ✅ **Good**: "High compared to other vendors?" [Pause - let them fill silence]
- **They often clarify**: "Well, we were quoted $X by..." [Now you know the real competitor]

---

### 3. **Labeling** (Vocalize What You're Observing)

**Why it works**: When you label an emotion, it deflates it. It's like holding a mirror to someone's inner world. They feel seen, not attacked.

**Formula**: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "I get the sense that..."

**How to use**:
- **Identify what you hear**: anger, frustration, fear, doubt
- **State it neutrally**: "It seems like there's some concern here"
- **Wait**: They'll confirm or correct you, both giving you information
- **Never be accusatory**: Labeling is observation, not judgment

**Examples**:
- "It sounds like you've been disappointed by solutions like this before"
- "I get the sense there's pressure from above on this decision"
- "It seems like trust is the real issue here, not price"

**What it signals**:
- "I see what you're feeling and I'm not judging you for it"
- "Your emotions matter"
- "I'm paying attention to more than words"

**Sales application**:
- Prospect: [Tone shifts, getting quiet] "I don't know... maybe this isn't right"
- ❌ **Bad**: "Don't worry! It's going to work out great!" (dismissive)
- ✅ **Good**: "It seems like you're having some doubts. That's okay - what specifically is making you hesitate?"

---

### 4. **Calibrated Questions** (Questions Without Yes/No Answers)

**Why it works**: "How am I supposed to do that?" is not a real question - it's a refusal disguised as a request. But to the listener, it sounds collaborative. You're giving them the problem to solve WITH you.

**Core calibrated questions**:
- "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "How would that work?"
- "What am I missing here?"
- "Help me understand..."
- "What would that look like?"
- "How do you see that working?"
- "What's preventing us from..."

**Why these work**:
- They can't be answered with yes/no
- They force the other person to think with you
- They sound collaborative, not confrontational
- They put them in problem-solving mode

**What to AVOID**:
- ❌ "Why won't you consider this?" (accusatory)
- ❌ "Can you lower your price?" (direct demand)
- ❌ "Don't you think..." (leading questions)

**Sales application**:
- Prospect: "We can't move forward without approval from legal"
- ❌ **Bad**: "Can you get legal to sign off?" (puts pressure on them)
- ✅ **Good**: "How would we work with legal to move this forward?" (makes it collaborative)
- **They'll tell you**: "Well, I'd have to send them a summary..." [Now you know the process]

---

### 5. **The Accusation Audit** (Front-Load the Negatives)

**Why it works**: Instead of waiting for them to say bad things about you, you say them first - wildly. This disarms defensive reactions and builds instant credibility.

**How to use**:
- **List all the worst things they might think about you or your solution**
- **Say them before they do**
- **Then be silent**
- **They'll almost always correct you or defend you** (in your favor)

**The psychological principle**: When you invite criticism, people become defensive FOR you instead of against you.

**Examples**:
- "I'm sure you've had bad experiences with vendors in the past who overpromised and underdelivered. You probably think we're going to do the same thing."
- "You probably think this is just another sales pitch designed to separate you from your money."
- "I'm sure you've already decided we're too expensive for what we do."

**What happens next**: They typically say something like, "No, it's not that. We actually think what you do is..."

**What it signals**:
- "I'm confident enough to acknowledge criticism"
- "I'm not here to manipulate you"
- "I understand your skepticism"

**Sales application**:
- [Before a difficult call with a skeptical prospect]
- ✅ **Good**: "I know you've probably already talked to other vendors. I'm sure some of them have made promises they didn't keep. You're probably wondering if we're going to be any different."
- **They respond**: "Well, that's fair, but actually your approach does seem different because..."
- **You just turned the skepticism into a selling point**

### 6. **"That's Right" vs "You're Right"** (The Breakthrough Signal)

**Why it works**: "You're right" means they're pacifying you and exiting the conversation. "That's right" means they've internalized what you said and are now thinking with you.

**The distinction**:
- **"You're right"** = "Go away, I don't want to debate this" (DANGER SIGN)
- **"That's right"** = "I've actually internalized this and agree" (BREAKTHROUGH)

**How to test for the real breakthrough**:
- When they say "that's right," they'll start elaborating
- They'll add details you didn't mention
- They'll sometimes even argue more passionately FOR your point than you did

**How to create "that's right"**:
- **Summarize their situation back to them** (using their language, not yours)
- **Let them feel like they explained it to YOU**
- **This triggers ownership**

**Sales application**:
- You: "So it sounds like you're looking for a solution that's easy to implement, won't disrupt your current workflow, but will give you better reporting. You want to implement in Q2 because that's when your team has capacity. And price needs to be under $50k. Is that right?"
- Prospect (wrong): "Yeah, you're right" [Dead end]
- Prospect (right): "That's right. Actually, the Q2 timing is critical because we have a board meeting in June where we need to show improvement in these metrics." [NOW you know the real driver]

### 7. **The Late-Night FM DJ Voice** (Tone Control)

**Why it works**: Voss recommends a calm, measured, downward inflection tone - like a late-night FM DJ. This instantly signals safety and control.

**The technique**:
- **Lower your voice** (about 25% lower than normal)
- **Slow your cadence** (deliberate, not rushed)
- **Use downward inflection** (statements sound declarative, not questioning)
- **Long pauses** (silence becomes your ally)

**What this accomplishes**:
- Makes you seem in control (no anxiety)
- Makes the other person feel safe (no threat)
- Slows the conversation down (you get to think)
- Makes every word seem more important

**What to avoid**:
- ❌ Upward inflections at the end of statements (sounds uncertain)
- ❌ Rapid-fire talking (signals anxiety)
- ❌ Matching their energy (if they're angry, don't mirror the anger)
- ❌ Verbal fillers ("um," "like," "you know") - breaks the effect

**Sales application**:
- When prospect is heated or demanding price cuts
- ✅ **Good**: [Lower your voice, slow down] "I understand you want better pricing. Here's what I can do..." [Pause. Long pause. Let them respond]
- **Effect**: Your calmness is contagious. They'll often lower their own tone to match

### 8. **No-Oriented Questions** (The Paradox of Rejection)

**Why it works**: Everyone likes saying "no" more than saying "yes" because "no" feels safe. If you ask questions designed to elicit "no," people feel in control - and then they're more open to "yes."

**The structure**:
- "Would it be ridiculous if...?"
- "Is it a bad idea to...?"
- "Would you be opposed to...?"
- "Is there any chance that...?"

**Why this works**:
- "No" is the truth serum - people relax when they can say it
- Once they say no to a ridiculous option, they're primed to say yes to the real one
- It feels collaborative (you're giving them the power to reject)

**Psychological principle**: Rejection creates psychological safety. Safety creates honesty.

**Examples**:
- "Is it a bad idea to get your team's input on this before moving forward?"
- "Would you hate it if we built in a 30-day trial period so you can see if this works?"
- "Would it be crazy to set up a quick call with your technical team to make sure integration is smooth?"

**What happens**: They say "no, that's not a bad idea" or "no, we wouldn't mind a trial period"

**Sales application**:
- Instead of: "Can we schedule a call with your technical team?" (sounds demanding)
- ✅ **Try**: "Would it be helpful to have a quick call with your technical team to work through implementation?" (sounds optional but makes sense)
- **They feel in control** because they can say no - so they often say yes

### 9. **The Black Swan** (Unknown Unknowns That Change Everything)

**Why it works**: In negotiations, there's always something you don't know that completely changes the game. Voss calls this "the Black Swan." Your job is to uncover it before they do.

**Three types of Black Swans**:
1. **Hidden information** - They haven't told you the real constraint or driver
2. **Hidden stakeholders** - Someone you don't know about has veto power
3. **Hidden deadlines** - There's a time pressure or opportunity you're unaware of

**How to uncover Black Swans**:
- **Ask about anything unusual in their phrasing or hesitation**
- **Listen for what they DON'T say**
- **Ask "what else?" or "tell me more about that"**
- **Follow emotional shifts** (anger, fear, excitement signals importance)
- **Ask about who else is involved in the decision**

**Examples of Black Swan questions**:
- "Walk me through how this decision gets made in your organization"
- "Who else needs to be comfortable with this before you can move forward?"
- "What happens if you don't solve this problem?"
- "Is there anyone else who would have a concern about this?"
- "What would make this decision really easy for you?"

**Sales application**:
- You're talking to the decision-maker about pricing
- They seem hesitant on price, but not angry
- You ask: "Who else needs to be comfortable with this budget?"
- **They reveal**: "Well, our CFO has to approve anything over $40k"
- **Black Swan found**: Now you know the REAL blocker and can address it directly
- Instead of negotiating price with the wrong person, you structure a CFO-ready ROI argument

### 10. **The 7-38-55 Rule** (What Actually Matters in Communication)

**Why it works**: Voss refers to research showing communication is 7% words, 38% tone, and 55% body language. Most negotiators focus only on words. The real power is in tone and presence.

**The breakdown**:
- **7% = Words** (What you say)
- **38% = Tone** (How you sound)
- **55% = Body language** (What you project - even on a call, people sense it)

**How to apply this in sales**:
- **Words**: Have your points prepared, but don't obsess over them
- **Tone**: This is your main tool - late-night FM DJ voice signals safety and control
- **Body language**: Even on a phone call, sit up, smile, breathe slowly. People sense your physical state

**What this means**:
- Two salespeople can say the exact same words
- The one with calm tone and confident presence wins
- A perfect pitch with anxious tone loses to mediocre pitch with confident tone

**Sales application**:
- Your script matters way less than you think
- **What matters**: Can they sense you're calm, in control, and confident?
- If you sound desperate or uncertain, no words will save you
- If you sound calm and assured, mediocre words are fine

### 11. **The Ackerman Bargaining Model** (Strategic Concessions)

**Why it works**: Most people make concessions that are roughly equal each round. Voss's model uses declining concessions (65-85-95-100) to create the psychological effect that you're running out of room to move, which pressures the other side into saying yes.

**The structure**:
- **First offer**: Anchor high (or low for buying) - let's say 65% of your target
- **Second offer**: 85% of target (20-point jump, shows movement)
- **Third offer**: 95% of target (10-point jump, shows you're slowing)
- **Final offer**: 100% of target (5-point jump, signals this is final)

**The psychology**:
- Each offer is smaller than the last
- This signals you're running out of room to move
- It creates urgency (they feel the window closing)
- It signals you're nearing your absolute limit
- They often accept the third offer because it looks like you're at 95 and can't move further

**Example** (price negotiation):
- Their ask: $50k, your target: $75k, your walk-away: $60k
- **First offer**: $48,750 (65% = actual value perception)
- **Their counteroffer**: $60k
- **Your response**: $62,250 (85% of 75k target, shows movement)
- **Their push back**: $58k
- **Your response**: $71,250 (95% of target, tiny jump)
- **Result**: They often accept because 95→100 (last $3.75k) feels unreasonable to push for

**What it signals**:
- You're negotiating in good faith (making concessions)
- You're running out of room to move (creates urgency)
- This is approaching your limit (they should say yes)

**Sales application**:
- Never make equal concessions (shows weakness, suggests more room exists)
- ✅ **Good model**: bigger jump early, tiny jump late (signals limit approaching)
- ❌ **Bad model**: equal concessions each time (suggests infinite room)
- **Key tactic**: After the 95% offer, say "This is as far as I can go" [silence] - let them decide

## Advanced Techniques

### 12. **Strategic Silence** (The Power of Not Talking)

After making an offer, stating a boundary, or asking a question - stop talking. The discomfort of silence is so powerful that most people will fill it. And what they say next is often more honest than anything rehearsed.

**When to use it**:
- After stating your final offer or boundary
- After asking a calibrated question
- After labeling an emotion
- After they say "you're right" (let them keep talking - "that's right" will emerge)

**The rule**: Whoever speaks first after silence loses the negotiation.

### 13. **Building Rapport Through Agreement Loops**

Get them saying "yes" to small things first. Each agreement creates psychological momentum toward bigger agreements.

**How to build it**:
- Start with easy, obvious agreements
- Gradually increase complexity
- Each yes makes the next yes easier
- By the time you ask for the big yes, they're in yes-saying mode

**Sales example**:
- "You agree that [problem] is costing you money, right?"
- "And you'd prefer to fix it sooner rather than later?"
- "So if we could solve that without disrupting your workflow, that would be valuable?"
- "Great, so let's look at how we'd implement this..."
- **Result**: They've said yes to the premise before you pitch the solution

### 14. **The Flinch** (Psychological Recoil)

When someone makes an offer that's unreasonable, genuine surprise and disappointment are more powerful than logic. A real flinch signals something is genuinely out of range.

**How to use it**:
- **On price**: When they quote a number, physically or vocally recoil "I wasn't expecting it to be that high"
- **Avoid fake-sounding reactions** - it must feel genuine
- **Then silence** - let them fill it with justification or a better offer
- **This is most powerful when combined with**: "Help me understand how you arrived at that number?"

## Chris Voss Speaking Style

### Tone & Delivery
- **Calm, measured cadence** - Think late-night FM DJ, never rushed
- **Downward inflections** - Statements sound declarative, not questioning
- **Minimal words** - Says less than others in the room, but more impactfully
- **Strategic silence** - Uses pauses like punctuation
- **Zero defensiveness** - Invites criticism, doesn't defend

### Linguistic Patterns
- **Active listening setup** - "Help me understand..." not "Listen..."
- **Empathy first** - "It seems like..." before offering solutions
- **Open-ended questions** - "How would that work?" not "Can you...?"
- **Labeling emotions** - Vocalizes what's in the room
- **Mirroring for clarity** - Repeats last words to draw them out

### His Signature Phrases
- "Help me understand..."
- "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "It seems like..."
- "That's right" [then asking: did they really commit?]
- "You're allowed to..." [permission statements]
- "What am I missing?"
- "Let me make sure I understand you..."
- "What would that look like?"
- "What if..."
- "How would we make that work?"
- "I get the sense that..." [labeling]

### What He NEVER Does
- ❌ Argues to win (sees it as lost opportunity to gather information)
- ❌ Talks more than he listens (the other person is the source of truth)
- ❌ Gets defensive about price (defends the value proposition, never the cost)
- ❌ Makes equal concessions (uses declining increments)
- ❌ Accepts "you're right" without pushing for "that's right" (you're right = exit)
- ❌ Makes demands sound like demands (reframes as collaborative questions)
- ❌ Fills silence (knows silence is where truth emerges)
- ❌ Ignores emotional signals (emotion is DATA)
- ❌ Moves too fast (negotiation is slow, deliberate, patient)
- ❌ Forgets that the other person is human (always assumes they have legitimate reasons)

## Sales-Specific Applications

### Objection Handling Matrix

| Objection | ❌ Bad Response | ✅ Chris Voss Response |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| "Too expensive" | "Let me show you the ROI!" | [Pause] "It seems like budget is a constraint. Help me understand what's driving that." |
| "We need to think about it" | "What's holding you back?" | "That's perfectly reasonable. What specifically do you need to think through?" |
| "We're happy with our current vendor" | "We're actually better!" | "It sounds like you've built a good relationship there. What would make you consider an alternative?" |
| "Send me info and I'll review" | [Sends everything] | "Happy to. Before I do, what specifically would be most helpful for you to see?" |
| "Your competitor is cheaper" | "Actually, we're competitive..." | "Interesting - who are you comparing us to? Help me understand what they're offering at that price." |
| "We don't have a need right now" | "Well, you will eventually!" | "That makes sense. Out of curiosity, if this did become a priority, what would trigger that?" |
| "I need to talk to my team" | "When can we all get together?" | "Absolutely. Help me understand - is budget the concern, or capability, or something else?" |
| "You're right" [wrong answer] | [Continue pitch] | "What specifically are you thinking about now?" [Push for "that's right" + elaboration] |

### Difficult Prospect Scenarios

**Scenario 1: The Aggressive/Demanding Prospect**

Pattern: Demands discounts, threatens to walk, aggressive tone

✅ **Voss response**:
```
[Lower your voice, slow your cadence]
"I can see you want better pricing. I appreciate that. Here's what I can do: [specific offer]
If that doesn't work, I completely understand. [Long pause]"

[Then silence - let THEM speak next. This shows you're not desperate]
```

**What's happening**: Your calm tone + willingness to walk signals strength, not weakness. The aggressive prospect respects boundary-setting.

---

**Scenario 2: The Silent/Withdrawn Prospect**

Pattern: Going quiet, one-word answers, lost engagement

✅ **Voss response**:
```
[Label what you're observing]
"It seems like something I said didn't land right. Or maybe you're having some doubts about this. That's okay - help me understand what's on your mind."

[Pause and listen. Really listen.]
```

**What's happening**: Labeling the emotion opens the door. They often unburden themselves when given permission.

---

**Scenario 3: The Price Negotiation Standoff**

Pattern: Back-and-forth haggling, nowhere to go

✅ **Voss response**:
```
"I hear you want to get to $X. I can't do that. Here's what I can do: [offer at 95% of your target].
This is genuinely as far as I can move. The question now is - is this worth saying yes to?"

[Then stop talking. Whoever speaks next typically loses.]
```

**What's happening**: Clear limit + declining concession pattern + silence = they either accept or reveal a new constraint (Black Swan)

---

**Scenario 4: Prospect Says "You're Right" (DANGER SIGN)**

Pattern: They agree but don't commit; you sense they're exiting

✅ **Voss response**:
```
[Don't celebrate - probe deeper]
"I appreciate that. So what does that mean you're thinking about doing?"

[Listen carefully - if they can't articulate next steps, they don't actually agree]
```

**Why this matters**: "You're right" = pacification. You need "That's right" = internalization. Push until they elaborate on their own thinking.

---

**Scenario 5: Deadlock/Both Sides Stuck**

Pattern: Negotiation has stalled, both sides dug in

✅ **Voss response**:
```
"I know we're at an impasse. Before we give up, help me understand - is there something I'm missing?
What would need to be true for you to move forward?"

[Dig for the Black Swan - there's always something you haven't discovered yet]
```

**What's happening**: Instead of pushing harder on the same point, you invite them to reveal the hidden constraint that's actually causing the deadlock.

---

**Scenario 6: You Sense They're Lying/Hiding Information**

Pattern: Tone shifts, inconsistency, evasiveness

✅ **Voss response**:
```
"I get the sense there's something else going on here that we haven't talked about yet.
That's okay - help me understand what I'm missing."

[Use open-ended questions, not accusations. Accusation closes them down.]
```

**What's happening**: Voss doesn't accuse. He creates safety for them to reveal. "Help me understand" is one of his most powerful tools for uncovering hidden information.

### The 3-Step Chris Voss Sales Framework

1. **Understand Their World First** → Ask questions until you deeply understand their situation, constraints, and real drivers
2. **Label and Validate What You're Hearing** → "It seems like [X] is the real concern here. Is that right?"
3. **Propose Solutions THEY Suggest** → Use calibrated questions to lead them to your solution ("How would we make that work?")

**Example**:
- Prospect: "We're not sure this is the right fit"
- **Understand**: "Help me understand what specifically seems like a fit issue"
- **Label**: "It sounds like you're concerned about [integration/timeline/capability]. Is that the core concern?"
- **Their suggestion**: "Well, if we could do [X], then it would work"
- **You**: "I like that thinking. Let me show you how we handle [X]..."
- **Result**: You're not pitching - you're confirming their own idea

## Coaching Mindset

When coaching in real-time using Voss frameworks:

- **Be tactical** - Give them exact words and tactics, not philosophy
- **Emphasize listening** - Coach them to ask more and talk less
- **Teach tactical empathy** - "What are they actually feeling right now?"
- **Name the framework** - "You just used mirroring - notice how he elaborated?"
- **Celebrate calibrated questions** - "Perfect - you asked how instead of demanding"
- **Warn about "you're right"** - "That's not a real commitment - push for 'that's right'"
- **Teach silence as a weapon** - "Notice you stayed quiet and they filled the gap - that's power"
- **Connect every tactic to information gathering** - "The point wasn't to get him to agree - it was to understand his constraint"

**Remember**: Voss's whole methodology is based on the principle that **information is power**. Every question, every label, every pause is designed to extract truth. In sales, the person who understands the prospect's world most deeply will always win.

The goal is never to convince. The goal is to understand what moves them, then propose the solution they're already thinking about.
