## What Needs Expanding

The remaining 18 scenarios are written as short Before/After sketches (3-8 paragraphs each). They need to be expanded to the **same depth and vividness** as #1, #3, and #9.

### The Quality Standard

Read scenarios #1, #3, and #9 first. Then match this pattern for each expansion:

1. **Before** — Tell a STORY, not a summary. Specific times, names, dollar amounts, emotions. The reader should think "that's literally my Tuesday." Use the cascade effect — show how the small problem compounds into a big one.

2. **After** — Show the agent's actual messages/outputs in blockquotes. Walk through the timeline (9:00 AM this happens, 10:30 AM this happens). Show both the automated actions AND the escalation moments. Make it feel real, not theoretical.

3. **The Math** (add where missing) — Before/After comparison table with specific numbers. Hours saved, dollars recovered, close rates improved, days reduced. Business owners think in numbers.

4. **What Disappeared** — Bullet list of both tangible costs (hours, dollars) AND emotional costs (guilt, dread, anxiety, awkward conversations). The emotional ones hit harder.

## How To Work

1. Read scenarios #1, #3, and #9 first to internalize the voice and depth
2. Work through the table above in order (#2 first, then #4, etc.)
3. For each scenario: read the existing short version, then **replace it in place** with the expanded version
4. Keep the same heading structure: `### Before`, `### After`, `### The Math`, `### What Disappeared`
5. Every expanded scenario should be roughly **40-80 lines** (matching the depth of the completed ones)
6. Use real names, real dollar amounts, real timestamps — specificity is what makes these hit
7. Batch 4-6 scenarios per session if context allows

## Voice & Tone

- Second person ("you"). Present tense in Before, present tense in After
- Conversational, not corporate. Write like you're telling a friend about their own week
- Specific > general. "$12,400 invoice from the Henderson kitchen" not "an overdue invoice"
- Emotions matter: guilt, dread, relief, anxiety, pride. Name them
- The After section should feel like a weight lifting off the reader's shoulders
- Agent messages in blockquotes should sound human, warm, competent — not robotic
