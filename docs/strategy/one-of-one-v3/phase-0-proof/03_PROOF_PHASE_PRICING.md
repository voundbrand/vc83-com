## Pricing Philosophy

**Earn the right to charge more later. The first 5 customers buy proof, not a product.**

Phase 0 pricing is not what the platform is worth. It's what removes friction for customers who are taking a bet on a solo founder with zero track record. The price should make saying yes trivially easy while still creating real commitment.

## Framing to Customer

> "You're getting founder-level attention at early-adopter pricing. This will cost 5x more in 6 months. I'm building case studies, and you're getting a product that large companies will pay €35K+ to implement."

Key framing principles:
- **They're early adopters, not discount customers.** Language matters. "Early-adopter pricing" signals exclusivity. "Discount" signals cheap.
- **Anchor at v3 pricing.** Always mention that the platform will cost €35K+ for setup alone. Makes €2-3K/month feel like a steal.
- **Founder-level attention is the premium.** They're not getting less — they're getting more. Direct access to the person who built it.
- **Time-limited by nature.** This pricing disappears after 5 customers. No coupon codes, no negotiation — it's structural.

## When to Raise Prices

### After Customer 3
- New customers pay €3-5K/month.
- Existing customers stay at their original rate (honor the early-adopter deal).
- You now have proof: "3 businesses are running on this right now."

### After Customer 5
- Introduce €5-10K setup fee.
- Monthly moves to €3-5K/month.
- Phase 0 is over. Phase 1 pricing reflects real market feedback.

### Phase 1 (5-15 customers)
- v3 pricing tiers begin to take shape.
- Setup fees: €5-12K depending on complexity.
- Monthly: €2-5K depending on agent count and usage.
- Annual contracts become an option (discount for commitment).

### Phase 2 (15+ customers)
- v3 tiers (€12K-€195K) become real.
- Pricing reflects case studies, proven ROI, and market positioning.
- Phase 0 customers offered upgrade path or grandfathered at reasonable rates.

## What NOT to Do

### Don't price at €499/month.
That's SaaS commodity pricing. It attracts the wrong customer — someone comparing you to Calendly or a basic chatbot. At €499/month, you're a tool. At €2K/month, you're a service. At €35K setup, you're a transformation. Phase 0 sits in the middle intentionally.

### Don't price at €35K setup.
You have zero proof, zero case studies, zero references. Nobody pays €35K to a solo founder they met last week. The v3 pricing is earned, not declared. Phase 0 earns it.

### Don't do free forever.
The 2-week trial is free. After that, €2K/month minimum. Free customers don't provide signal — they don't churn because they never committed. €2K/month creates real commitment and real feedback. If they won't pay €2K after seeing it work for 2 weeks, the product or the fit isn't there.

### Don't negotiate below €2K/month.
€2K is the floor. Below that, the unit economics don't work even for Phase 0, and you signal that the product isn't worth real money. If someone pushes for less, offer a shorter trial or a narrower scope — not a lower price.

## Margin Note

At €2-3K/month with near-zero infrastructure costs, margins are 90%+. The platform runs on:
- ElevenLabs voice (usage-based, pennies per call)
- Convex backend (current plan covers Phase 0 scale)
- Vercel hosting (current plan covers Phase 0 scale)
- Domain and basic SaaS tools (~€100/month)

The main cost is Remington's time. At 5 customers, the revenue covers a modest living in Berlin and funds continued development. It doesn't fund a team, an office, or marketing spend — and it doesn't need to yet.
