## What Kai Customers Produce for v3

Every Phase 0 Kai customer generates proof artifacts that the v3 mid-market strategy cannot exist without. Different artifacts than Track A — and that is exactly the point.

### 1. Team Adoption Data

Clara proves that AI can answer phones. Kai proves that entire teams will adopt AI into daily workflows.

When 15 employees message Kai daily for vacation requests, shift swaps, and availability checks, that is a fundamentally different proof point than one owner watching Clara take calls. Team adoption data answers the mid-market buyer's biggest question: "Will my people actually use this?"

Metrics to collect:
- Time to first team-wide adoption (how many days until >80% of team uses Kai)
- Daily active users as a percentage of team size
- Fallback rate (how often people revert to WhatsApp/phone/Excel instead of Kai)
- Organic expansion (do people start asking Kai questions that weren't part of the original scope?)

### 2. Ops Pain Case Studies

Track A produces case studies about missed calls and revenue recovery. Track B produces case studies about operational efficiency and time savings.

The v3 mid-market pitch needs both:
- "We eliminated 10 hours/week of manual scheduling for a 12-person team" resonates with COOs, ops directors, and HR leads — buyers who don't care about phone agents but deeply care about operational overhead.
- "Kai prevented 3 scheduling conflicts in the first month that would have left a location understaffed" resonates with multi-location operators who have been burned by coordination failures.

These are different stories for different buyers at the same target company. The COO cares about ops. The owner cares about revenue. Track A and Track B produce proof for both.

### 3. Platform Expansion Proof

The v3 strategy is a multi-agent platform, not a single-agent tool. The biggest risk to v3 pricing (EUR 35K-195K) is: "We'll just buy the phone agent."

When a Kai customer adds Clara, or a Clara customer adds Kai, that is direct proof that the platform expansion motion works. It validates:
- Customers buy the second agent (upsell works)
- The platform value exceeds any single agent's value
- Multi-agent pricing is justified because customers use multiple agents

This is the single most important proof point for v3. Without it, platform pricing is a hypothesis. With it, platform pricing is a demonstrated business model.

### 4. Different Buyer Network

Track A reaches owners and front-desk managers. Track B reaches ops managers, HR leads, and team coordinators.

These are different people at different companies — AND different people at the same company. In a mid-market organization with 200 employees:
- The ops director might champion Kai
- The customer service director might champion Clara
- Both report to the COO, who buys the platform

Two entry points into the same organization. Two internal champions. Two paths to a platform deal. This is how multi-agent enterprise sales actually works.

Phase 0 is where all of this gets built.

## Cross-Track Synergy

### Track A Customer Adds Kai

A business that bought Clara to answer phones discovers they also have team coordination chaos. Adding Kai proves:
- The platform expands beyond the initial use case
- Customers see value in multiple agents
- Revenue per customer increases (validates v3 ARPA targets)

### Track B Customer Adds Clara

A business that bought Kai for vacation/shift management discovers they are also missing phone calls. Adding Clara proves:
- The Kai wedge opens the full platform (not just a scheduling tool)
- Different entry points converge on the same platform
- The "seven specialists" narrative is real, not marketing

### Either Direction Validates v3

It does not matter which direction the expansion goes. What matters is that it happens. One cross-sell in Phase 0 is worth more to the v3 strategy than three same-track customers, because it proves the platform model.

## Phase 0 to Phase 1 Gate (Shared with Track A)

The gate criteria apply across both tracks combined. Track A and Track B are not separate businesses — they are two sales motions for one platform.

- [ ] **3+ paying customers retained 2+ months** (across both tracks combined)
- [ ] **2+ case studies with permission to use name/logo/data** (at least one from each track ideal, but not required)
- [ ] **Kai handles 90%+ of coordination requests without manual intervention** (Track B equivalent of Track A's call automation criterion)
- [ ] **One clear pattern emerged** — which wedge converts faster? Which vertical closes easiest? Which objection kills deals?
- [ ] **EUR 10K+ MRR combined** (Track A + Track B)

If all four gates are met, Phase 0 is complete. Transition to Phase 1 regardless of the track split.

## The Strategic Insight

> "If Track B (Kai) converts faster than Track A (Clara), that doesn't mean we pivot away from voice. It means we lead with ops and expand into voice. The platform is the same. The wedge is just the door.
>
> And if both tracks convert at similar rates, that's the best outcome of all — it means the platform has two proven entry points, two sets of case studies, and two buyer networks. That makes the v3 multi-agent platform pitch twice as credible."

The purpose of running two tracks is not to pick a winner. It is to learn which doors open fastest, for which buyers, with which pain points. That intelligence is what transforms Phase 0 guesswork into Phase 1 conviction.
