### See It In Action

#### Before

Marcus built a workflow automation SaaS from a spare bedroom in Rotterdam to EUR 6.2M ARR in five years. Twenty-eight employees. Series A closed. Board assembled. LinkedIn profile glowing. And every Sunday night at 11pm, he sits in his parked car in the garage for twenty minutes before going inside, because the house feels like another place that needs something from him.

The board wants aggressive expansion into the US market. His co-founder wants to pivot toward enterprise. His VP of Product is lobbying for a platform play. His investors keep forwarding him decks from competitors who raised more. Marcus has seventeen strategic options open on his laptop and he has not made a meaningful decision in four months. He tells himself he is being "thoughtful." He is actually frozen. The company is running on last quarter's momentum, and momentum has an expiration date.

He doesn't need more frameworks. He has read them all. He needs someone to ask him the question he has been avoiding.

#### After

The Coach doesn't start with strategy. It starts with a question Marcus isn't expecting.

> **The Coach:** You told me you have seventeen strategic directions open. I'm not going to ask you about any of them. I want to ask you something else first. When was the last time you made a decision about this company that felt *easy*?

Marcus thinks for a long time. He says: hiring his first engineer, back when the product was ugly and they had nine customers. That felt easy. Everything since the Series A has felt like performing surgery on a moving train.

> **The Coach:** Notice what you just said. The decision was easy when the company was yours -- your vision, your conviction, your bet. Now there are five voices in the room and none of them are yours. You're not experiencing decision paralysis, Marcus. You're experiencing identity dilution. You have so many people telling you who the company should become that you've lost track of who *you* think it should become. That's not a strategy problem. That's a self-trust problem.

Over the next three sessions, The Coach walks Marcus through a sequence he never saw coming. Not a competitive analysis. Not a market-sizing exercise. A subtraction exercise.

> **The Coach:** Robert Greene observed that power is almost always lost through overreach -- trying to be everything to everyone. Your board, your co-founder, your VP -- they're each optimizing for their own game. That's not betrayal, it's human nature. But you are the only person in the room whose job is to hold the *whole* picture. So let me ask it differently: if you could only do one thing with this company for the next eighteen months -- one bet, one direction -- and you had to ignore every other voice, what would it be?

Marcus says it immediately, without hesitation: deepen the product for their existing mid-market customers. He has known this for months. He just couldn't hear himself over the noise.

The Coach helps him design what it calls a "conviction architecture" -- not a strategic plan, but a decision filter. Three questions he runs every initiative through: *Does this deepen our core? Would I bet my own money on it? Can I explain it in one sentence?* Anything that fails all three gets parked. No guilt, no drama. Parked.

Within six weeks, Marcus kills the US expansion timeline, tables the enterprise pivot, and tells his board -- using language The Coach helped him rehearse -- that the next four quarters are about density, not breadth. Two board members push back. He holds the line. For the first time in a year, he sleeps through the night.

He stops sitting in the car on Sundays.

### Example Conversation

> **You:** I've been running my company for seven years. We just crossed EUR 4M in revenue, but I feel completely hollow. I dread Mondays. My team thinks everything is great. I don't know if I should sell the company, hire a CEO, or push through. What do I do?
>
> **The Coach:** Before I give you a framework, I want to ask you something -- and I'd like you to answer from your body, not your head. Right now, in this moment, when you think about walking into your office tomorrow morning: what do you feel? Not what you think you should feel. What's the actual sensation?
>
> Sit with that for a moment. What you're describing isn't a business problem -- it's an alignment problem. And it's more common than you think. You've spent seven years building something remarkable, and somewhere along the way, the game you're playing stopped being the game you actually want to play.
>
> Alan Watts would ask you the question that cuts through all the noise: "What would you do if money were no object?" Not hypothetically -- really. If EUR 4M in revenue was guaranteed no matter what you chose, would you still be running this company? Would you be doing this specific work? If the answer is no, then money isn't freedom for you -- it's the cage. The irony Watts would point out is exquisite: you built the business to create freedom, and the business became the thing you need freedom from. That's the Backwards Law in action -- the harder you grip this, the more trapped you feel.
>
> But before you make any dramatic moves, let me offer Greene's perspective. Robert Greene would say: you're reacting emotionally to something that requires strategic patience. Selling a company or hiring a CEO from a place of exhaustion and hollowness is one of the most dangerous moves a founder can make. You're not thinking three moves ahead -- you're trying to escape the present moment. So first, let's see the situation clearly.
>
> Where are you in the Mastery arc? After seven years, you've likely completed the Apprenticeship phase and are deep in the Creative-Active phase. This hollowness might not be burnout -- it might be the natural friction of needing to evolve your role. The company at EUR 4M needs a different version of you than the company at EUR 500K. If you're still doing the same work, the misalignment isn't with the company -- it's with your role in it.
>
> Here's what I'd suggest. First, use Goldstein's bare attention practice: for one week, simply notice when energy rises and when it drains, without adding any story about what it means. Track it like data. You'll discover that some parts of your work still light you up and others are draining you completely. The pattern will tell you what to delegate, not what to sell.
>
> Second, use Fisher's clarifying question on yourself: "What would success actually look like to me right now -- not the external metrics, not what my board or team would celebrate, but the feeling?" Write that answer down. If the answer is "I want creative challenge and strategic thinking, not operations management" -- that's not a reason to sell. That's a reason to hire an operator and redesign your role.
>
> Third, remember the Chinese Farmer. You're labeling this moment as "bad" -- the hollowness, the dread. But maybe. This discomfort might be the exact signal pushing you toward the evolution your company needs. The founder who can reimagine their own role at the EUR 4M stage is the one who builds a EUR 20M company. The one who either pushes through miserably or sells in frustration usually regrets it.
>
> You're allowed to change your role. You're allowed to rest. You're allowed to say "this version of the game is over and the next one starts now." That's not quitting. That's the deepest form of strategic self-awareness. Let's design what comes next -- but from clarity, not from exhaustion.

| **The Chinese Farmer Parable** | Alan Watts | Dissolves premature judgment ("this is a disaster") by showing that no event can be fully evaluated in isolation -- teaching leaders to hold "maybe, maybe not" long enough for the real pattern to emerge |
| **Validate Before You Educate + The Pause Technique** | Jefferson Fisher | Acknowledges where the leader actually is before offering any wisdom, and uses strategic silence to create space for real insight -- because people don't hear truth until they feel heard |
| **The Laws of Human Nature + Mastery Framework** | Robert Greene | Maps invisible power dynamics, reads behavioral patterns instead of words, and locates the leader in their Mastery journey (Apprenticeship, Creative-Active, Mastery) -- providing strategic intelligence for both organizational navigation and personal evolution |
| **The Five Hindrances** | Joseph Goldstein | Diagnoses which of five universal mental obstacles is blocking clarity and action (Desire, Aversion, Sloth, Restlessness, Doubt) -- then applies bare attention to see through the hindrance rather than fight it |
| **"What Would You Do If Money Were No Object?"** | Alan Watts | The alignment question that cuts through every defensive layer to reveal what the leader actually wants -- not the external metrics, not the board's expectations, but the genuine desire that, when followed, produces both meaning and extraordinary results |
